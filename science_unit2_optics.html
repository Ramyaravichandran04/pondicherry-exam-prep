Unit 2: Optics

INTRODUCTION TO LIGHT

What is Light?

Light is a form of energy which travels in the form of waves.

The path along which light travels is called a ray of light.

A group of light rays is called a beam of light.

Source of Light

Any object which gives out light is called a source of light.

Objects that produce their own light are called luminous objects.

Example: Sun, stars

We can see objects only when:

  • Light falls on the object
  • The object reflects light
  • Reflected light enters our eyes

Without light, we cannot see.

PROPERTIES OF LIGHT

  • Light is a form of energy.
  • Light travels in a straight line.
  • Light does not need a medium (can travel in vacuum).
Speed of light in vacuum: c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s

Light has:

  • Wavelength (λ)
  • Frequency (ν)
c = νλ

Different colours have different wavelengths.

  • Violet → Lowest wavelength
  • Red → Highest wavelength

When light falls on two media → Partly reflected & partly refracted.

REFLECTION OF LIGHT

A highly polished surface like a mirror reflects most of the light falling on it.

Laws of Reflection

First Law: The angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection.

∠i = ∠r

Second Law:

The incident ray, reflected ray and normal at the point of incidence lie in the same plane.

These laws apply to all reflecting surfaces, including spherical mirrors.

Image Formation by Plane Mirror

The image formed by a plane mirror is:

  • Virtual
  • Erect
  • Same size as object
  • Laterally inverted
  • Formed at same distance behind mirror as object is in front

CURVED MIRRORS

Curved mirrors form images with different characteristics.

Example: Viewing your face in a shining spoon:

  • Inner surface → concave mirror
  • Outer surface → convex mirror

SPHERICAL MIRRORS

A spherical mirror is a mirror whose reflecting surface forms a part of a sphere.

Types of Spherical Mirrors

  • Concave Mirror: A spherical mirror whose reflecting surface is curved inward.
  • Convex Mirror: A spherical mirror whose reflecting surface is curved outward.

Pole (P) - The centre of the reflecting surface of a mirror.

Centre of Curvature - The centre of the sphere of which the mirror surface forms a part.

  • In a concave mirror → in front of mirror
  • In a convex mirror → behind mirror

Radius of Curvature (R)

R = PC

Principal Focus (F)

When parallel rays of light fall on a mirror, they either converge at a point or appear to diverge from a point. This point is called the principal focus.

  • Concave Mirror: Parallel rays meet at the focus.
  • Convex Mirror: Parallel rays appear to diverge from focus.

Focal Length (f)

f = PF

Aperture of Mirror – The diameter of the reflecting surface.

Aperture << Radius of curvature

Relation Between Radius of Curvature and Focal Length

R = 2f

Focus lies midway between pole and centre of curvature.

Uses of Concave Mirrors

  • Torches
  • Search lights
  • Vehicle headlights
  • Shaving mirrors
  • Dentist mirrors
  • Solar furnaces

Convex Mirrors

  • Always form virtual image
  • Erect image
  • Diminished image

Convex mirrors are used as rear-view mirrors in vehicles.

MIRROR FORMULA

1/v + 1/u = 1/f

Where

  • u = Object distance
  • v = Image distance
  • f = Focal length

Magnification

Magnification (m) tells how large or small the image is compared to the object.

  • m positive → virtual image
  • m negative → real image

REFRACTION OF LIGHT

When light travels from one transparent medium to another, its path bends. This bending is called Refraction.

Reason: Speed of light changes in different media.

  • Faster in rarer medium
  • Slower in denser medium

Laws of Refraction

First Law

The incident ray, refracted ray and normal lie in the same plane.

Second Law (Snell’s Law)

sin i / sin r = μ₂ / μ₁
μ = c / v

μ – refractive index

c – speed of light in vacuum

v – speed of light in medium

Refractive Index

Refractive index describes how much light bends in a medium.

  • Fastest in vacuum
  • Slightly slower in air
  • Much slower in water or glass

If higher refractive index → lower speed of light.

Bending Rules

  • Rarer → Denser → bends towards normal
  • Denser → Rarer → bends away from normal

DISPERSION OF LIGHT

Splitting of white light into its seven colours when passed through a prism is called dispersion.

Spectrum colours:

VIBGYOR

  • Violet
  • Indigo
  • Blue
  • Green
  • Yellow
  • Orange
  • Red

Red → least deviation

Violet → maximum deviation

SCATTERING OF LIGHT

When light enters atmosphere and is redirected in all directions by particles, it is called scattering.

The particle causing scattering is called scatterer.

Types of Scattering

Elastic Scattering

Energy before and after scattering remains same.

Inelastic Scattering

Energy of incident beam and scattered beam are different.

  • Rayleigh scattering
  • Mie scattering
  • Tyndall scattering
  • Raman scattering

Rayleigh Scattering

Scattering of sunlight by atoms or molecules in the atmosphere.

S ∝ 1 / λ⁴

Mie Scattering

Occurs when particle size is similar to wavelength of light.

Responsible for white appearance of clouds.

Tyndall Effect

Scattering of light by colloidal particles.

Example: Sunlight entering a dusty room.

Examples of colloids:

  • Milk
  • Ice cream
  • Muddy water
  • Smoke

Raman Scattering

Interaction of light with particles causing change in wavelength.

Types of spectral lines:

  • Rayleigh Lines
  • Raman Lines

Raman lines are classified into:

  • Stokes Lines
  • Anti-Stokes Lines

LENSES

A lens is a transparent medium bounded by two spherical surfaces or one plane surface.

Types

  • Convex Lens
  • Concave Lens

Convex Lens

Thicker at centre and converges light.

Concave Lens

Thinner at centre and diverges light.

Other Types

  • Plano-convex lens
  • Plano-concave lens

IMAGE FORMATION – CONVEX LENS

  • Object at infinity → Image real, small, at F
  • Beyond C → Real, inverted, smaller
  • At C → Real, same size
  • Between F and C → Real, enlarged
  • At F → Image at infinity
  • Between F and O → Virtual, enlarged

Applications

  • Camera lenses
  • Magnifying glass
  • Microscope
  • Telescope
  • Slide projector
  • Correct hypermetropia

IMAGE FORMATION – CONCAVE LENS

  • Always forms virtual image
  • Smaller in size
  • Formed between O and F

Applications

  • Eye lens of Galilean telescope
  • Door spy holes
  • Correct myopia

LENS FORMULA

1/f = 1/v − 1/u

Sign Convention

  • Object on left
  • Right direction → Positive
  • Left direction → Negative
  • Upward → Positive
  • Downward → Negative

Magnification

If magnification > 1 → enlarged image

If magnification < 1 → diminished image

OPTICAL DENSITY

The optical density of a medium refers to how much it slows down light.

  • Higher refractive index → optically denser
  • Lower refractive index → optically rarer

POWER OF LENS

Power represents ability of lens to converge or diverge light.

P = 1 / f

Unit: Dioptre (D)

1 D = 1 m⁻¹
  • Convex lens → positive power
  • Concave lens → negative power

HUMAN EYE

The eyeball is approximately spherical with diameter 2.3 cm.

Main parts:

  • Cornea
  • Iris
  • Pupil
  • Retina
  • Lens
  • Ciliary muscles
  • Optic nerve

Power of Accommodation

The ability of eye to focus near and distant objects.

Persistence of Vision

If time between images is less than 1/16 second, the eye cannot distinguish them separately.

Near Point

Least distance of distinct vision = 25 cm

Far Point

Maximum distance for normal eye = Infinity

DEFECTS OF VISION

Myopia

  • Short sightedness
  • Cannot see distant objects
  • Corrected using concave lens

Hypermetropia

  • Long sightedness
  • Cannot see near objects
  • Corrected using convex lens

Presbyopia

Occurs due to ageing and weakening of ciliary muscles.

Corrected using bifocal lenses.

Astigmatism

Cannot see horizontal or vertical lines clearly.

Corrected using cylindrical lens.

MICROSCOPE

Simple Microscope

  • Single convex lens
  • Virtual enlarged image

Compound Microscope

  • Two lenses
  • Magnification: 50 – 200 times

Travelling Microscope

Used for measuring very small lengths accurately.

Least count = 0.01 mm

TELESCOPE

Invented by Johann Lippershey (1608).

Types

  • Refracting telescope
  • Reflecting telescope

Examples of reflecting telescopes:

  • Gregorian
  • Newtonian
  • Cassegrain

Astronomical Telescope

Used to view stars, planets, galaxies and satellites.

Terrestrial Telescope

Used to view objects on Earth.

Main difference: terrestrial telescope gives erect image.
