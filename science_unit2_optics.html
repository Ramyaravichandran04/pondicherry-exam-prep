Unit 2: Optics

Introduction to Light

What is Light?

Light is a form of energy which travels in the form of waves.

The path along which light travels is called a ray of light.

A group of light rays is called a beam of light.

Source of Light

Any object which gives out light is called a source of light.

Objects that produce their own light are called luminous objects.

Examples: Sun, stars

We can see objects only when

  • Light falls on the object
  • The object reflects light
  • The reflected light enters our eyes

Without light, we cannot see.

Properties of Light

  • Light is a form of energy.
  • Light travels in a straight line.
  • Light does not need a medium and can travel in vacuum.
Speed of light in vacuum c = 3 × 108 m/s

Light has two important properties:

  • Wavelength (λ)
  • Frequency (ν)
c = νλ

Different colours have different wavelengths.

  • Violet → Lowest wavelength
  • Red → Highest wavelength

Reflection of Light

A highly polished surface like a mirror reflects most of the light falling on it.

Laws of Reflection

  1. Angle of incidence is equal to angle of reflection.
    2. ∠i = ∠r
  2. The incident ray, reflected ray and normal lie in the same plane.

Image Formation by Plane Mirror

  • Virtual image
  • Erect image
  • Same size as object
  • Laterally inverted
  • Formed at same distance behind mirror

Spherical Mirrors

A spherical mirror is a mirror whose reflecting surface forms a part of a sphere.

Types

  • Concave Mirror: Reflecting surface curved inward
  • Convex Mirror: Reflecting surface curved outward

Important Terms

  • Pole (P): Centre of reflecting surface
  • Centre of Curvature: Centre of the sphere
  • Radius of Curvature: Distance PC
R = 2f

Uses of Concave Mirror

  • Torches
  • Vehicle headlights
  • Shaving mirrors
  • Dentist mirrors
  • Solar furnaces

Uses of Convex Mirror

  • Rear-view mirrors in vehicles

Refraction of Light

When light travels from one transparent medium to another, its path bends. This is called refraction.

Laws of Refraction

  1. Incident ray, refracted ray and normal lie in the same plane.
  2. Snell's Law
sin i / sin r = μ₂ / μ₁

Refractive index

μ = c / v

Bending Rules

  • Rarer → Denser : bends towards normal
  • Denser → Rarer : bends away from normal

Dispersion of Light

Splitting of white light into seven colours using a prism is called dispersion.

The colours of spectrum are:

  • Violet
  • Indigo

Lenses

A lens is a transparent medium bounded by spherical surfaces.

Types

  • Convex Lens (Converging lens)
  • Concave Lens (Diverging lens)

Lens Formula

1/f = 1/v - 1/u

Power of Lens

P = 1 / f

SI Unit: Dioptre (D)

  • Convex lens → Positive power
  • Concave lens → Negative power

Human Eye

The eyeball is approximately spherical with diameter about 2.3 cm.

Main Parts

  • Cornea
  • Iris
  • Pupil
  • Retina
  • Lens
  • Ciliary muscles
  • Optic nerve

Power of Accommodation

The ability of the eye lens to focus near and distant objects.

Persistence of Vision

If two images appear within 1/16 second, the eye sees them as one continuous image.

Defects of Vision

Myopia (Short Sightedness)

  • Cannot see distant objects
  • Corrected using concave lens

Hypermetropia (Long Sightedness)

  • Cannot see near objects
  • Corrected using convex lens

Presbyopia

Occurs due to ageing.

Corrected using bifocal lenses.

Astigmatism

Corrected using cylindrical lens.

Microscope

Simple Microscope

  • Single convex lens
  • Virtual enlarged image

Compound Microscope

  • Two lenses
  • Magnification: 50–200 times

Types

Astronomical Telescope

Used to observe stars, planets and galaxies.

Terrestrial Telescope
