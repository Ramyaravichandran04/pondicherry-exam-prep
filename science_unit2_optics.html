🔬 Unit 2: Optics

🌟 INTRODUCTION TO LIGHT

✅ What is Light?

Light is a form of energy which travels in the form of waves.

The path along which light travels is called a ray of light.

A group of light rays is called a beam of light.

💡 Source of Light

Any object which gives out light is called a source of light.

Objects that produce their own light are called luminous objects.

Example: Sun, stars

We can see objects only when:

  • Light falls on the object
  • The object reflects light
  • Reflected light enters our eyes

Without light, we cannot see.

✨ PROPERTIES OF LIGHT

  • Light is a form of energy.
  • Light travels in a straight line.
  • Light does not need a medium (can travel in vacuum).
Speed of light in vacuum: c = 3×10^8  m/s

Light has:

  • Wavelength (λ)
  • Frequency (ν)
They are related by: c = νλ

Different colours have different wavelengths.

  • Violet → Lowest wavelength
  • Red → Highest wavelength

When light falls on two media → Partly reflected & partly refracted.

🪞 REFLECTION OF LIGHT

A highly polished surface like a mirror reflects most of the light falling on it.

Laws of Reflection:

First Law: The angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection.

∠i=∠r

Second Law: The following lie in the same plane: Incident ray, Reflected ray, Normal at the point of incidence.

These laws apply to all reflecting surfaces, including spherical mirrors.

Image Formation by Plane Mirror:

The image formed by a plane mirror is:

  • Virtual
  • Erect
  • Same size as object
  • Laterally inverted
  • Formed at same distance behind mirror as object is in front

🥄 Curved Mirrors

Curved mirrors form images with different characteristics.

Example: Viewing your face in a shining spoon:

  • Inner surface → concave mirror
  • Outer surface → convex mirror

🪞 SPHERICAL MIRRORS

A spherical mirror is a mirror whose reflecting surface forms a part of a sphere.

Types of Spherical Mirrors

Concave Mirror: A spherical mirror whose reflecting surface is curved inward.

Convex Mirror: A spherical mirror whose reflecting surface is curved outward.

Pole (P) - The centre of the reflecting surface of a mirror.

Centre of Curvature - The centre of the sphere of which the mirror surface forms a part.

It lies outside the mirror.

In a concave mirror → in front of mirror

In a convex mirror → behind mirror

Radius of Curvature - Radius of Curvature (R)

R=PC

Principal Focus (F)

When parallel rays of light fall on a mirror, they either: Converge at a point, or Appear to diverge from a point. This point is called the principal focus.

Concave Mirror: Parallel rays meet at the focus.

Convex Mirror: Parallel rays appear to diverge from focus.

Focal Length (f) = Distance between: Pole (P) and Focus (F)

f=PF

Aperture of Mirror: The diameter of the reflecting surface of a spherical mirror.

Relation Between Radius of Curvature and Focal Length

R=2f

Focus lies midway between pole and centre of curvature.

Uses of Concave Mirrors

  • Torches
  • Search lights
  • Vehicle headlights
  • Shaving mirrors
  • Dentist mirrors
  • Solar furnaces (to concentrate sunlight)

Convex mirrors always form

  • Virtual image
  • Erect image
  • Diminished image

Convex mirrors are used as: Rear-view mirrors in vehicles

📏 Mirror formula

1/v+1/u = 1/ f

where Object distance (u), Image distance (v), Focal length (f)

Magnification

Magnification (m) tells how large or small the image is compared to the object.

m positive → virtual image

m negative → real image

🌈 REFRACTION OF LIGHT

Definition: When light travels from one transparent medium to another, its path bends. This bending is called Refraction.

Reason: Speed of light changes in different media.

Faster in rarer medium

Slower in denser medium.

📘 Laws of Refraction

1️⃣ First Law

The incident ray, refracted ray of the light, and normal to the refracted surface all lie in the same plane.

2️⃣ Second Law (Snell’s Law)

sin⁡i/sin⁡r = μ2/μ1
μ = c/v

μ - refractive index

c - speed of light in vacuum

v - speed of light in a medium

Refractive Index

Refractive index describes how much light bends in a medium.

Speed of light in vacuum: 3×10^ 8 m/s

Light travels:

  • Fastest in vacuum
  • Slightly slower in air
  • Much slower in water or glass

If Higher refractive index → then lower speed of light

📘 Bending Rules

Rarer → Denser → bends towards normal drawn to the interface

Denser → Rarer → bends away from normal drawn to the interface

🌈 DISPERSION OF LIGHT

Definition: Splitting of white light into its 7 colours when passed through prism is called dispersion.

Spectrum colours: VIBGYOR

  • Violet
  • Indigo
  • Blue
  • Green
  • Yellow
  • Orange
  • Red

Different colours bend by different amounts.

Red → least deviation

Violet → maximum deviation

🔬 MICROSCOPE

Simple Microscope

  • Single convex lens
  • Virtual enlarged image

Compound Microscope

  • Two lenses
  • High magnification (50–200 times)

Travelling Microscope: A travelling microscope is one of the best instrument for measuring very small length with high degree of accuracy at the order of 0.01mm.

Least count = 0.01 mm

🔭 TELESCOPE

Invented by Johann Lippershey (1608)

Types:

  • Refracting telescope
  • Reflecting telescope

Astronomical Telescope: An astronomical telescope is used to view heavenly bodies like stars, planets galaxies and satellites.

Terrestrial Telescopes: The image in an astronomical telescope is inverted. So, it is not suitable for viewing objects on the surface of the Earth. Therefore, a terrestrial telescope is used.

The major difference between astronomical and terrestrial telescope is erecting the final image with respect to the object.
